Academic Research · Master’s Thesis

Mapping Boreal Forest Resilience and Ectomycorrhizal Fungi in Scandinavia

An integrated geospatial study of forest change, soil moisture resilience, and post-fire recovery in Scandinavian boreal forests.

Overview

This master’s thesis explored how GIS and remote sensing can be used to investigate ectomycorrhizal fungi (EMF), boreal forest resilience, and climate-related disturbance in Scandinavia. The study brought together forest ecology and geospatial analysis to examine how remote sensing can contribute to understanding forest composition, environmental stress, and ecosystem recovery over time.

The thesis was developed through three connected case studies focused on forest change in Trysil, Norway, soil moisture resilience during extreme weather periods, and post-fire recovery in Enskogen, Sweden.

Research Focus

The work examined how remote sensing could be used to analyse forest structure and resilience in relation to ectomycorrhizal fungal associations and broader ecological function. A central aim was to assess whether satellite-based methods could help identify spatial patterns relevant to forest health, climate adaptation, and environmental change.

Study Components

1. Forest Change in Trysil, Norway

The first part of the thesis investigated long-term tree species and forest-type change over time in Trysil. This included analysis of associations between changing forest cover and ectomycorrhizal fungi-linked forest systems.

2. Soil Moisture Resilience

The second component explored how boreal forest areas responded to extreme weather years through soil moisture analysis. This part of the study focused on identifying areas that appeared more resilient under climatic stress.

3. Post-Fire Recovery in Enskogen, Sweden

The final component examined post-fire recovery using remote sensing-based change detection. The objective was to assess vegetation recovery patterns and compare burned and recovering areas over time.

Data and Methods

The project combined multiple geospatial datasets and remote sensing methods to investigate forest dynamics across space and time.

Key tools and datasets: ArcGIS Pro, SNAP, Landsat, Sentinel-1 SAR, Sentinel-2, LiDAR-supported reference data, vegetation and moisture indices, supervised classification, SVM classification, and change detection methods.

Why This Research Matters

Boreal forests are important for biodiversity, carbon storage, and climate regulation. Understanding how they respond to drought, fire, and long-term ecological change is highly relevant for both conservation and climate mitigation.

This project shows how GIS and remote sensing can support environmental monitoring by helping identify resilient areas, track vegetation change, and produce spatial evidence for ecological interpretation and planning.

Key Takeaways

The study demonstrated that geospatial methods can provide useful insight into changing forest conditions, moisture dynamics, and recovery after disturbance. It also highlighted the importance of scale, classification quality, and ground-truthing when interpreting ecological patterns from satellite-based analysis.
